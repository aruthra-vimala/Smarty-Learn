Newton's Second Law Experiment

Newton's Second Law of Motion states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This law can be expressed with the following equation:

F = ma

Where:

  • F is the net force acting on the object (measured in Newtons, N).
  • m is the mass of the object (measured in kilograms, kg).
  • a is the acceleration produced by the force (measured in meters per second squared, m/s¬≤).

This law explains how the velocity of an object changes when a force is applied to it. The greater the force applied to an object, the greater its acceleration will be, provided that the mass of the object remains constant.

Understanding the Relationship:

- When the force acting on an object increases, its acceleration increases if the mass stays the same.

- When the mass of an object increases, the acceleration decreases for the same amount of force.

Examples:

  • Example 1: If you push a cart, the cart will accelerate in the direction of the applied force. The harder you push, the faster the cart accelerates.
  • Example 2: If you apply the same amount of force to two objects, one heavier and one lighter, the lighter object will accelerate more than the heavier one because it has less mass.
