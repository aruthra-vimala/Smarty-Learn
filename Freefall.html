Free Fall Experiment

Free fall is the motion of a body under the influence of gravity alone, with no other forces acting on it, such as air resistance. It is a fundamental concept in physics, often used to understand how objects move when dropped from a height.

In free fall, all objects accelerate towards the Earth (or any other planet or celestial body) at the same rate, regardless of their mass. This acceleration due to gravity is typically denoted as g, and on Earth, its value is approximately 9.8 m/s¬≤.

Key Concepts of Free Fall:

Acceleration due to Gravity (g): The constant acceleration that objects experience when in free fall. On Earth, g is approximately 9.8 m/s¬≤.

Initial Velocity: If an object is dropped from rest, its initial velocity is zero. If it is thrown downwards, it will have a non-zero initial velocity.

Time of Fall: The duration for which an object falls depends on the initial velocity and the height from which it is dropped.

Example of Free Fall:

If you drop an object from a height of 20 meters, its motion will be affected only by gravity (assuming air resistance is negligible). The object will accelerate downwards at 9.8 m/s¬≤, and the velocity will increase until it hits the ground.

Equations for Free Fall:

The following kinematic equation can be used to calculate the velocity or distance fallen:

v = u + gt
Where: v = final velocity (m/s)
u = initial velocity (m/s)
g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s¬≤)
t = time of fall (seconds)
